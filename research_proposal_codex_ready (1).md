# Backward GVC Participation and Export Exchange-Rate Elasticity in C26 Manufacturing: A Comparison with Broader Manufacturing, with a Focus on China and Malaysia

*Research Proposal*

**Research field:** International Economics / Global Value Chains / Exchange Rates / Trade

**Core design:** Country-industry-year panel; OECD TiVA + BIS REER; C26 as the core sector; China and Malaysia as focal cases for interpretation

## 1. Introduction

The relationship between exchange rates and exports has long been central to international economics. In conventional trade theory, a real depreciation is expected to stimulate exports by improving price competitiveness in foreign markets. However, this mechanism becomes less straightforward once production is organised through global value chains (GVCs). In such settings, exporters rely not only on domestic inputs but also on imported intermediate goods. Exchange-rate movements therefore affect exports through two channels at the same time: a competitiveness channel and an imported-input cost channel. When the foreign value-added content embodied in exports is high, a depreciation may improve the price competitiveness of the domestic value-added part of exports while simultaneously increasing the cost of imported intermediates.

Existing research has already established the broad mechanism behind this problem. Ahmed et al. (2015) show that GVC participation reduces the real effective exchange-rate (REER) elasticity of manufacturing exports and that backward linkages are especially important in this process. Their argument is intuitive: if a larger share of exports embodies imported inputs, a depreciation improves competitiveness only for the domestic part of exports, while imported-input costs move in the opposite direction. In this sense, the standard export-competitiveness story becomes less complete in a more fragmented production setting.

Later work suggests that even this aggregate mechanism is still only part of the story. de Soyres et al. (2021) argue that export responses depend not only on the direct bilateral exchange rate between exporter and importer, but also on where the value added is produced and where it is ultimately absorbed. Adler et al. (2023) similarly show that GVC integration dampens trade-flow elasticities, even though exchange rates still remain relevant for external adjustment overall. Together, these studies suggest that it is no longer sufficient to ask only whether GVC

participation matters. It is also necessary to consider how value-chain structure, sourcing patterns, and external-adjustment conditions shape the size of export responses.

Recent evidence on product sophistication and ICT exports sharpens the industry-level question further. Chen et al. (2023) find that more sophisticated Chinese exports are less responsive to exchange-rate changes, while Chen et al. (2026) report a similar pattern for China's ICT exports and also show that tariffs have stronger effects than appreciations for highly sophisticated ICT products. This implies that broad manufacturing averages may not be enough to explain sectors whose production is more technologically complex and more deeply embedded in cross-border production networks.

For this reason, C26 manufacturing provides a useful next step. C26 covers computer, electronic, and optical products, which are closely tied to electronics and semiconductor-related production. OECD (2024) shows that, in the Philippine case, the foreign value-added share of gross exports is higher in C26 than in broader manufacturing, and notes that this foreign value-added intensity is commonly treated as a measure of backward linkages. This does not prove that the same pattern must hold in every economy, but it does make C26 a theoretically plausible and empirically researchable setting for asking whether backward GVC participation matters more there than in broader manufacturing.

## 2. Problem Statement

Although the literature has already shown that GVC participation weakens export exchange-rate elasticity at the aggregate manufacturing level, three issues remain insufficiently addressed.

First, much of the existing evidence is still presented at the broad manufacturing or aggregate-country level. This is useful for establishing the main mechanism, but it leaves limited room to observe whether the same mechanism operates with different intensity across industries.

Second, while backward linkages are known to matter, fewer studies directly compare whether their moderating role is stronger in a specific industry than in the broader manufacturing sample. In other words, the literature has been more successful in proving the existence of the mechanism than in comparing its relative intensity across sectors.

Third, the move from product-sophistication and ICT evidence to a clearly defined manufacturing category remains underdeveloped. Existing studies already suggest that more sophisticated goods are less exchange-rate responsive and that East Asian electronics production

is strongly shaped by imported intermediates and regional supply chains. Thorbecke (2018) shows that electronics exports in ASEAN are closely tied to electronics imports and supply-chain deepening, while Xing (2012) shows that China's processing imports are heavily concentrated in East Asia and support finished exports to third markets. These patterns strongly suggest that a sector such as C26 should not be treated as if it were equivalent to average manufacturing.

Accordingly, the problem addressed by this study is not whether GVC participation weakens export exchange-rate elasticity in general. That point is already well established in the literature. The narrower and more researchable problem is whether the dampening effect of backward GVC participation is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing, and how this pattern can be interpreted in the cases of China and Malaysia, given their different industrial structures and forms of imported-input dependence. Put differently, the study shifts the discussion from proving the existence of the mechanism to examining its relative intensity in a more fragmented and technologically complex industry setting.

## 3. Research Questions

1. What is the baseline relationship between real effective exchange-rate movements and industry-level exports?

2. Does backward GVC participation weaken export exchange-rate elasticity at the industry level?

3. Is this dampening effect stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing?

4. How can the observed pattern be interpreted in the cases of China and Malaysia?

## 4. Research Objectives

General objective: To examine whether backward GVC participation dampens export exchange-rate elasticity more strongly in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.

**Specific objectives:**

1. To estimate the relationship between real effective exchange-rate movements and industry-level exports.

2. To test whether backward GVC participation weakens export exchange-rate elasticity at the industry level.

3. To compare whether this dampening effect is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.

4. To interpret the observed pattern with a focus on China and Malaysia within a broader country-industry framework.

## 5. Literature Review

Research on exchange rates and exports usually begins with the competitiveness channel. In its simplest form, a real depreciation should stimulate exports by lowering the relative price of domestic goods in foreign markets. However, this relationship has never been equally strong across all countries or all sectors. Even before bringing global value chains into the discussion, the literature already suggests that export elasticities depend on more than relative prices alone. Production structure, sourcing patterns, and the organisation of trade also matter. For the present study, this means the relevant issue is not simply whether exchange rates affect exports, but under what production conditions that effect becomes weaker or stronger.

The rise of global value chains makes this issue more systematic. Once exports embody imported intermediates, a depreciation no longer operates only through price competitiveness. It may improve the competitiveness of the domestic value-added part of exports, but it also raises the cost of imported inputs used in production. In that sense, GVC participation does not remove the exchange-rate channel; rather, it makes the standard expenditure-switching story less complete. This is the broad manufacturing mechanism established by Ahmed et al. (2015), who show that GVC participation reduces the REER elasticity of manufacturing exports and that backward linkages are especially important in this process. Their contribution is central to this study because it provides the aggregate baseline on which the present analysis builds, rather than a result that needs to be demonstrated again. Ahmed et al. (2015) further report that GVC participation lowers the elasticity of manufacturing exports to the REER by about 22 percent on average, with stronger effects in more highly integrated cases.

Later studies suggest that even this aggregate mechanism is still only part of the story. The issue is not simply how much foreign value added is embodied in exports, but also how production is organised across countries. de Soyres et al. (2021) make this point clearly by arguing that exports react not only to the direct bilateral exchange rate between exporter and importer, but to the exchange-rate relationship between the country producing the value added and the country of final absorption. This matters because foreign value added, re-imported domestic value added, and re-exported intermediates do not affect export responsiveness in the same way. Adler et al. (2023) push the discussion further by placing the argument in an external-adjustment framework.

Their contribution is useful because it shows that GVC integration dampens trade-flow elasticities without making exchange rates irrelevant. In other words, exchange rates still matter, but their effects become more conditional on production structure and imported-input dependence.

Seen together, these studies imply that broad manufacturing estimates may conceal meaningful variation across industries. Once value-chain structure, sourcing patterns, and imported-input dependence become central, it becomes difficult to assume that a highly fragmented sector should behave in the same way as the manufacturing average. This is where product sophistication and sectoral heterogeneity become relevant. Chen et al. (2023) show that China's more sophisticated exports are less responsive to exchange-rate movements, and their estimates indicate a clear gradient in which lower-sophistication products display more negative exchange-rate elasticities than highly sophisticated products. Chen et al. (2026) report a similar result for ICT exports, while also showing that tariffs have stronger effects than appreciations for more sophisticated ICT products. The key implication is not only that sophisticated products tend to have weaker exchange-rate responses, but that export composition itself can alter the apparent strength of exchange-rate effects. This makes the manufacturing average a less satisfactory benchmark when the sector under study is more technology-intensive and more deeply embedded in cross-border production networks.

However, moving from ICT and product-sophistication evidence to C26 still requires one more step. The reason is that C26 is not simply a convenient label for "high-tech" production. It refers more specifically to computer, electronic, and optical manufacturing, which is closely related to electronics and semiconductor production. Evidence from East Asian supply chains helps make this transition more concrete. Thorbecke (2018) shows that electronics exports in Asian supply chain economies are tightly linked to electronics imports, which indicates that imported inputs are central to export production in this sector. Xing (2012) similarly shows that China's processing imports are heavily concentrated in East Asia and are closely tied to finished exports destined for third markets. These studies do not test C26 directly, but they make it much easier to see why an electronics-related manufacturing category should not be treated as equivalent to average manufacturing.

This is why C26 becomes a reasonable next step rather than a conceptual jump. OECD (2024) identifies C26 as the category covering computer, electronic, and optical products, and its

discussion of semiconductor-related industries also treats foreign value-added intensity as a useful indicator of backward linkages. That point matters for the present study because it links the earlier literature on GVC participation and imported-input dependence to a clearly defined sectoral setting. In other words, the move from broad manufacturing to C26 is not based only on the intuition that C26 is "more complex". It is based on the stronger claim that this sector is more likely to combine imported-input dependence, dense production fragmentation, and lower short-run substitutability, all of which should make the dampening role of backward participation more visible.

The literature therefore does not leave a gap about whether GVC participation matters in general. That part is already well established. The more specific gap is whether the moderating role of backward GVC participation is especially strong in C26 manufacturing relative to broader manufacturing, and whether this pattern can be interpreted more clearly in the cases of China and Malaysia. This is the question taken up in the present study, and it leads directly to the conceptual framework and empirical strategy that follow.

## 6. Conceptual Framework and Hypotheses

The conceptual logic of this study begins with the standard relationship between exchange rates and exports. A real appreciation is generally expected to reduce exports by weakening price competitiveness in foreign markets. However, this relationship becomes less straightforward when industries rely more heavily on imported intermediate inputs. In that case, exchange-rate movements do not affect exports only through the relative price of final goods. They also affect production costs through the imported-input channel. The implication is not that exchange rates stop mattering, but that the size of export responsiveness becomes conditional on the degree of backward integration.

This study further argues that the moderating role of backward GVC participation may be stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing. The reason is not simply that C26 is more technologically advanced, but that it is more likely to be embedded in dense electronics and semiconductor production networks, where imported inputs, cross-border fragmentation, and lower short-run substitutability are more common. In such a setting, the competitiveness gains from depreciation are more likely to be offset by rising imported-input costs. The conceptual expectation, therefore, is that backward participation will weaken the exchange-rate elasticity of exports more strongly in C26 than in the broader manufacturing sample.

**Hypotheses:**

H1: Real effective exchange-rate appreciation is associated with lower industry-level exports.

H2: Higher backward GVC participation weakens the negative effect of REER appreciation on exports.

H3: The moderating role of backward GVC participation is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.

H4: The observed moderating pattern differs in interpretation between China and Malaysia because their industrial structures and forms of imported-input dependence are not identical.

## 7. Methodology / Research Design

This study adopts a quantitative empirical design based on annual country-industry panel data. The empirical setting is different from a pure China-Malaysia comparison. Instead, the study uses a broader country-industry framework, where C26 manufacturing is treated as the core sector and broader manufacturing is used as the comparison benchmark. China and Malaysia are then discussed as focal cases within this wider framework. The methodology follows the same general logic used in recent applied trade studies: it first defines the baseline empirical relationship, then introduces the channel suggested by the literature, and finally extends the model to fit the more specific research question. Jamil et al. (2026), for example, develop their empirical strategy by starting from an existing production-function setting, explaining the bias or channel that may be missing, and then adding the relevant terms step by step. This study follows that style of presentation, but the model itself is not copied from that article because the present research is not estimating firm-level productivity. The focus here is industry-level export exchange-rate elasticity under different degrees of backward GVC participation. The primary data sources are OECD TiVA for industry-level exports and backward GVC participation, and BIS effective exchange-rate data for REER measures (OECD, 2025; Bank for International Settlements, n.d.). The unit of analysis is the country-industry-year observation. The dependent variable is the logarithm of industry-level gross exports. The main explanatory variable is the logarithm of the real effective exchange rate. The moderating variable is lagged backward GVC participation, measured by the foreign value-added share embodied in gross exports. Additional macroeconomic controls may be included if necessary and if data coverage permits.

### 7.1 Baseline export exchange-rate elasticity model

The first step is to estimate the baseline relationship between exchange-rate movements and exports. This baseline model is useful because it gives the average export response to REER movements before the GVC channel is introduced:

$$
\ln X_{c,i,t} = \alpha + \beta_1 \ln REER_{c,t} + \mu_{c,i} + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{c,i,t}.
$$

(1)

where c denotes country, i industry, and t year. X_{c,i,t} refers to industry-level exports, and REER_{c,t} is the real effective exchange rate. Country-industry fixed effects, µ_{c,i}, control for time-invariant differences in industrial structure and export specialisation, while year fixed effects, λ_t, absorb common global shocks. If a higher REER indicates real appreciation, β1 is expected to be negative because appreciation should reduce export price competitiveness.

### 7.2 Introducing backward GVC participation

The baseline model is then extended by introducing backward GVC participation. This step is adapted from the GVC-exchange-rate elasticity literature, especially Ahmed et al. (2015), where imported-input dependence is expected to weaken the standard exchange-rate competitiveness channel. The model is written as:

ln X_{c,i,t} = α+β1 ln REER_{c,t} +β2 BGV Cc,i,t−1 +β3 (ln REER_{c,t} ×BGV Cc,i,t−1 )+µ_{c,i} +λ_t +ε_{c,i,t} .
(2)

Here, BGVC_{c,i,t-1} refers to lagged backward GVC participation. Lagging this variable helps reduce the simultaneity concern that current exports and current imported-input intensity may be jointly determined. The interaction term is the key part of this model. It tests whether the export response to REER movements changes when an industry relies more heavily on foreign value-added inputs. The marginal effect of REER on exports can be written as:

∂ ln X_{c,i,t}
= β1 + β3 BGV Cc,i,t−1 .                             (3)
∂ ln REER_{c,t}

If β1 is negative, REER appreciation reduces exports on average. If β3 is positive, the negative effect becomes weaker when backward GVC participation is higher. This would be consistent with the argument that imported intermediate inputs partly offset the normal competitiveness effect of exchange-rate movements.

### 7.3 C26 sector-comparison extension

The main contribution of this study is not only to test whether backward GVC participation matters, but to examine whether its dampening role is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing. To test this more directly, the preferred specification is a pooled triple-interaction model:

ln X_{c,i,t} = α + γ1 ln REER_{c,t} + γ2 BGV Cc,i,t−1
+ γ3 (ln REER_{c,t} × BGV Cc,i,t−1 )
+ γ4 (ln REER_{c,t} × C26_i ) + γ5 (BGV Cc,i,t−1 × C26_i )                 (4)
+ γ6 (ln REER_{c,t} × BGV Cc,i,t−1 × C26_i )
+ µ_{c,i} + λ_t + ε_{c,i,t} .

C26_i is a sector indicator equal to one for C26 manufacturing and zero for the comparison manufacturing group. The standalone C26_i term is not included separately because it is time-invariant and would be absorbed by the country-industry fixed effects. This is a cleaner specification than simply adding a C26 dummy to a fixed-effects model. In this model, γ3 captures the moderating effect of backward GVC participation in the comparison manufacturing group, while γ6 captures whether this moderating effect is additionally stronger or weaker in C26. Therefore, the main test for H3 is whether the triple-interaction coefficient supports a stronger dampening effect in C26. The corresponding marginal effect of REER on exports is:

∂ ln X_{c,i,t}
= γ1 + γ3 BGV Cc,i,t−1 + γ4 C26_i + γ6 (BGV Cc,i,t−1 × C26_i ).            (5)
∂ ln REER_{c,t}

This expression makes the interpretation clearer. For broader manufacturing, where C26_i = 0, the REER elasticity is γ1 + γ3 BGVC_{c,i,t-1}. For C26 manufacturing, where C26_i = 1, the REER elasticity becomes γ1 + γ4 + (γ3 + γ6 )BGVC_{c,i,t-1}. Therefore, γ6 directly indicates whether backward GVC participation changes the REER elasticity more strongly in C26 than in the comparison group.

### 7.4 Robustness checks and research boundaries

As a robustness check, the baseline moderation model can also be estimated separately for C26 and for the broader manufacturing benchmark, and the interaction coefficients can then be compared. The broader manufacturing benchmark should ideally exclude C26 when the data structure allows, so that the comparison is cleaner. Alternative lag structures for backward GVC participation may also be tested if the sample size is sufficient. China and Malaysia will be used as focal cases for interpretation rather than as the full sample. This allows the study to preserve a broader comparative design while still discussing whether the estimated mechanism appears especially meaningful for these two economies within Asian production networks. The study also has clear boundaries. It remains at the industry level because TiVA-based backward participation is naturally measured at the country-industry level. Product-level trade data and industry-level GVC measures do not align perfectly. As a result, the design provides structural evidence about sectoral differences in moderation strength, but it does not claim a fully causal

identification strategy.

## 8. Significance of the Study

This study is significant in three main ways. First, it moves the discussion from the existence of the mechanism to its relative intensity across industries. Rather than asking again whether GVC participation dampens export exchange-rate elasticity, it asks whether this dampening effect is especially strong in a specific industry setting. Second, the study helps connect three strands of literature that are often discussed separately: the aggregate GVC-elasticity literature, the value-chain-structure literature, and the product-sophistication / ICT literature. In doing so, it provides a clearer explanation for why broad manufacturing averages may be insufficient when production is more fragmented and technologically complex. Third, the study offers a structured way to interpret China and Malaysia within a broader Asian production-network context. China is deeply tied to East Asian processing and supply-chain structures, while Malaysia remains an important economy in regional electronics production. This makes the focal-case discussion more meaningful than a purely abstract comparison.
