# Backward GVC Participation and Export Exchange-Rate Elasticity in C26 Manufacturing: Evidence from a Country-Industry Panel with China and Malaysia as Focal Cases

*Research Proposal*

- **Research field:** International Economics; Global Value Chains; Exchange Rates; International Trade
- **Proposed design:** Annual country-industry panel using OECD TiVA and BIS REER data
- **Core sector:** C26 manufacturing, defined as computer, electronic, and optical products
- **Comparison group:** Broader manufacturing, preferably excluding C26 where data structure permits
- **Focal cases for interpretation:** China and Malaysia

## 1. Introduction

Exchange rates are usually discussed through a fairly direct competitiveness story. If a country’s real exchange rate depreciates, its exports should become cheaper to foreign buyers; if it appreciates, exports should become less competitive. That logic is still useful, but it becomes harder to apply without qualification when production is split across countries. Many exporters do not rely only on domestic inputs. They also import parts, components, and other intermediate goods before producing for foreign markets.

This matters because the same exchange-rate movement can work in more than one direction. A depreciation may improve the price competitiveness of the domestic value-added part of exports, but it can also raise the domestic-currency cost of imported inputs. An appreciation may weaken final-goods competitiveness, while lowering the cost of imported components. For industries with high imported-input dependence, the final export response may therefore be smaller than the standard model would suggest. In this proposal, that imported-input dependence is captured through backward GVC participation, measured as the foreign value-added share embodied in exports.

The broad mechanism is already well developed in the literature. Ahmed, Appendino, and Ruta (2015) show that GVC participation reduces the real effective exchange-rate (REER) elasticity of manufacturing exports, especially through backward linkages. Their result gives the starting point for this research rather than the whole research question. Once that general effect is accepted, a further issue becomes important: whether the dampening effect is equally strong across manufacturing industries.

C26 manufacturing is a useful sector for asking this question. It covers computer, electronic, and optical products, and it is closely connected to electronics and semiconductor-related

production. These activities often involve cross-border sourcing, specialized components, and regional production networks. For that reason, C26 may not behave like average manufacturing when exchange rates change. Evidence on Chinese product sophistication and ICT exports also points in this direction. Chen, Salike, and Thorbecke (2023) find weaker exchange-rate responsiveness among more sophisticated Chinese exports, while Chen, Salike, and Thorbecke (2026) report a similar pattern for China’s ICT exports. These studies do not directly answer the C26 question, but they make the sector a reasonable place to examine more closely.

China and Malaysia are included as focal cases because they are both important in Asian production networks, but not in the same way. China has a long history of processing trade and has also moved into more sophisticated stages of production. Malaysia remains strongly connected to electronics and semiconductor-related value chains, often through multinational production networks and imported components. Looking at both countries can help interpret the panel results in a more concrete way, without turning the whole project into a simple two-country comparison.

The main question, then, is not whether GVC participation matters at all. That has already been shown. The question is whether backward GVC participation weakens export exchange-rate elasticity more strongly in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing, and how this pattern can be understood for China and Malaysia.

### 1.1 Sectoral Motivation for Focusing on C26 Manufacturing

The choice of C26 manufacturing needs some explanation because it would be too simple to treat it only as a “high-tech” category. In this proposal, C26 is important not because high-technology sectors are automatically special, but because this sector is likely to combine several features that matter for exchange-rate responsiveness. It includes computer, electronic, and optical products, which are closely related to electronics and semiconductor-linked production. These activities often rely on imported components, specialized intermediate inputs, and production stages distributed across different economies.

This production structure changes the way exchange rates may affect exports. In a less fragmented sector, a depreciation can be discussed mainly through the price of final exports. In C26, that channel may be weaker because exporters may also face higher costs for imported components when the domestic currency depreciates. The final effect can therefore be less clear than in a simple competitiveness model. The exchange rate still matters, but its effect is filtered through the structure of input sourcing.

Another reason for focusing on C26 is its connection to regional production networks, especially in East Asia. Electronics and semiconductor-related production often involves cross-border flows of parts and components before a final product is exported. This is relevant for both China and Malaysia. China has been deeply involved in processing trade and electronics assembly, while Malaysia has an important position in electronics and semiconductor-related

production networks. These two cases are not identical, but both make the imported-input channel visible.

C26 also provides a clearer sectoral test than using broad labels such as “advanced manufacturing” or “technology-intensive exports”. The sector is defined in the TiVA industry structure, so it can be linked to both export values and foreign value-added shares. That makes it possible to test the research question within a consistent country-industry-year panel rather than relying only on descriptive claims about high-technology production.

For these reasons, C26 is not used here as a decorative case. It is the sector where the theoretical mechanism is expected to be more visible: imported inputs are important, production is often regionally fragmented, and export competitiveness may depend less on final-price changes alone. The comparison with broader manufacturing is therefore central. If backward GVC participation dampens exchange-rate elasticity more strongly in C26, it would suggest that the aggregate manufacturing result hides meaningful sectoral differences.

There is also a timing issue that makes C26 interesting. In electronics-related production, firms may not be able to change suppliers quickly when exchange rates move. Components are often specialized, quality requirements can be strict, and production relationships may be organized through longer-term supply-chain arrangements. Because of this, a short-run depreciation may not allow exporters to replace imported inputs with domestic inputs immediately. The imported-input cost channel can remain important even when the exchange-rate movement would normally be expected to improve export competitiveness.

This does not mean that all C26 exports are insensitive to exchange rates. The sector includes different products, firms, and stages of production. Some parts of the sector may still compete strongly on price. The point is more careful: C26 is a sector where the usual price-competitiveness channel is likely to be mixed with imported-input costs and value-chain constraints. That makes it a good test case for whether backward GVC participation changes export exchange-rate elasticity more strongly than in broader manufacturing.

## 2. Problem Statement

The existing literature gives a strong explanation for why GVC participation can weaken the exchange-rate elasticity of exports. What is less clear is whether that effect is stronger in some industries than in others.

Much of the evidence is still presented at the aggregate manufacturing level or at the country level. This helps establish the basic mechanism, but it can hide differences between industries. A sector that uses many imported components may respond differently from a sector that relies more heavily on domestic inputs. A broad manufacturing estimate may therefore be too general for industries such as electronics-related manufacturing.

There is also a gap in how backward linkages are compared across sectors. Backward

GVC participation is recognized as important, but fewer studies ask whether its moderating effect is stronger in one sector than in the wider manufacturing benchmark. In other words, the literature has done more to show that imported-input dependence matters than to compare where it matters most.

The move from ICT and product-sophistication evidence to C26 also needs a clearer empirical design. Studies on sophisticated exports suggest weaker exchange-rate responsiveness, and studies on Asian electronics production show the importance of imported inputs and regional supply chains. Thorbecke (2018), for example, links ASEAN electronics exports closely with electronics imports. Xing (2012) shows how China’s processing imports from East Asia support exports to third markets. These findings suggest that C26 should not simply be treated as average manufacturing, but the point still needs to be tested in a country-industry framework.

The research problem is therefore narrower than the general GVC question. It asks whether the dampening effect of backward GVC participation is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing, and how this sectoral pattern can be interpreted for China and Malaysia.

### 2.1 Empirical Gap and Research Positioning

The empirical gap addressed in this proposal is specific. It is not enough to repeat that GVC participation weakens export exchange-rate elasticity. That point has already been made clearly in the existing literature, especially by Ahmed et al. (2015). The more useful question is whether the same dampening mechanism has the same strength across sectors.

This distinction matters for the research design. If the project only tested whether backward GVC participation moderates the REER-export relationship, it would mainly reproduce an established argument with a different sample. The proposed contribution comes from comparing C26 with broader manufacturing. In other words, the project asks whether the imported-input channel is more important in a sector where imported components, electronics supply chains, and regional production fragmentation are more central.

The positioning of the study is therefore sectoral rather than purely general. It builds on the aggregate GVC-exchange-rate literature, but it does not stop there. It also uses the product-sophistication and ICT export literature to justify why C26 may behave differently from average manufacturing. At the same time, it avoids making the project too narrow by using a broader country-industry panel instead of only China and Malaysia.

China and Malaysia are used to interpret the results because they help make the sectoral mechanism more concrete. They are not treated as the only empirical sample, and the study does not claim that two countries can represent all C26 production. Their role is more limited and more useful: they help explain what the estimated relationship may mean in economies that are strongly connected to Asian electronics production networks.

This positioning keeps the proposal manageable. The study does not need to prove every part of the GVC literature again. It takes the main mechanism as a foundation and asks a narrower empirical question: whether the dampening effect of backward GVC participation is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.

The empirical positioning also affects how the results should be judged. A significant interaction between the REER and backward GVC participation would not be enough by itself, because that would mainly support the general GVC argument. The more important result is the comparison between the interaction effect in C26 and the same effect in broader manufacturing. This is why the triple-interaction model is necessary. It allows the thesis to ask whether the moderation effect is different in the sector that theory suggests should be more exposed to imported-input dependence.

In this sense, the project is not trying to make a very broad claim about all high-technology exports or all Asian production networks. It makes a more limited claim that can be tested with available data. If the results support the hypothesis, the contribution will be to show that the GVC dampening mechanism is not uniform across manufacturing. If the results do not support the hypothesis, that would also be useful because it would suggest that C26 may not be as different from broader manufacturing as expected, or that the relevant differences are not captured well by the available industry-level data.

## 3. Research Questions

1. What is the baseline relationship between real effective exchange-rate movements and
industry-level exports?

2. Does backward GVC participation weaken the exchange-rate elasticity of industry-level
exports?

3. Is the dampening effect of backward GVC participation stronger in C26 manufacturing
than in broader manufacturing?

4. How can the observed pattern be interpreted in the cases of China and Malaysia?
## 4. Research Objectives

The general objective is to examine whether backward GVC participation dampens export exchange-rate elasticity more strongly in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.

The specific objectives are:

1. To estimate the baseline relationship between REER movements and industry-level exports.
2. To test whether backward GVC participation moderates the relationship between REER
movements and industry-level exports.

3. To compare the strength of this moderating effect between C26 manufacturing and
broader manufacturing.

4. To interpret the empirical pattern with specific reference to China and Malaysia within a
broader country-industry framework.

## 5. Literature Review

### 5.1 Exchange Rates, Exports, and Imported Inputs

The starting point is the traditional exchange-rate channel. A real depreciation is expected to increase exports by improving price competitiveness, while a real appreciation is expected to reduce exports. This view is useful, but empirical export responses are rarely uniform. They vary by country, industry, product type, and production structure.

One reason is that many exporters are also importers. Amiti, Itskhoki, and Konings (2014) show that firms engaged in both importing and exporting may have weaker exchange-rate pass-through because input costs and export prices are linked inside the same production process. Although their work is at the firm level, the idea is relevant here: imported inputs can make exchange-rate effects less direct.

At the industry level, the same logic suggests that export elasticities should not be treated as fixed. A sector that relies mainly on domestic inputs may react more strongly to exchange-rate changes than a sector whose exports contain a large foreign value-added component. This is the point that connects the exchange-rate literature with GVC measures.

### 5.2 GVC Participation and Export Elasticity

Ahmed et al. (2015) provide the main reference for the link between GVCs and exchange-rate elasticity. They find that GVC participation lowers the REER elasticity of manufacturing exports, with backward linkages playing a particularly important role. The mechanism is straightforward: if exports include more imported inputs, a depreciation does not only make final exports more competitive; it can also make those imported inputs more expensive.

This result is important for the current research because it gives a clear empirical foundation. The purpose here is not to prove from zero that GVC participation matters. Instead, the proposal builds on that result and asks whether the same mechanism is stronger in a specific manufacturing sector.

The use of backward participation is also practical. Foreign value added embodied in exports gives a measurable way to capture imported-input dependence. It is not a perfect measure of all supply-chain complexity, but it is directly connected to the channel being studied.

### 5.3 Value-Chain Structure

Some later work shows that the issue is not only how much foreign value added is used, but also where production and final demand are located. de Soyres, Frohm, Gunnella, and Pavlova (2021) argue that export responses depend on exchange-rate relationships along the value chain, not only on the direct exporter-importer exchange rate. Adler, Meleshchuk, and Osorio Buitron (2023) make a related point in the context of external adjustment: GVC integration can dampen trade-flow elasticities, even though exchange rates still matter.

These studies make the sectoral question more credible. If the structure of production changes how exports respond to exchange rates, then it is not enough to look only at manufacturing as a whole. Industries with more fragmented production should be examined separately.

### 5.4 Product Sophistication, ICT, and C26

Research on product sophistication adds another reason to focus on C26. Chen et al. (2023) find that China’s more sophisticated exports are less responsive to exchange-rate changes. Chen et al. (2026) report a similar pattern for China’s ICT exports and show that tariffs can matter more than appreciations for highly sophisticated ICT products.

These findings do not mean that all high-technology exports behave in the same way. They do, however, suggest that export composition matters. More sophisticated goods may compete less through simple price changes and more through technology, quality, supply-chain position, or contractual relationships. Since C26 includes computer, electronic, and optical products, it is a suitable industry category for examining this issue in a more structured way.

### 5.5 East Asian Electronics Production

The East Asian evidence is especially relevant because C26 production is often regionally organized. Thorbecke (2018) finds that ASEAN electronics exports are closely related to electronics imports, which points to the importance of imported components in export production. Thorbecke (2019) also discusses exchange rates in East Asian value chains. Xing (2012) shows that China’s processing imports from East Asia are connected to its finished exports to third markets.

These studies help explain the choice of China and Malaysia as focal cases. China has combined processing trade, large-scale assembly, and increasing domestic upgrading. Malaysia has a strong role in electronics and semiconductor-related production, with continued reliance on imported inputs and multinational networks. They are not identical cases, but both are useful for interpreting the same empirical mechanism.

### 5.6 Research Gap

Taken together, the literature shows that GVC participation can weaken exchange-rate elasticity, that value-chain structure affects export responses, and that sophisticated or ICT-related exports may be less sensitive to exchange-rate changes. The remaining gap is more specific: there is still room to test whether backward GVC participation has a stronger dampening effect in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.

That is the gap addressed here. The contribution is not a new theory of exchange rates. It is a more focused sectoral comparison using C26 as the core industry, broader manufacturing as the benchmark, and China and Malaysia as focal cases for interpretation.

### 5.7 Synthesis of the Literature

The literature can be read as a sequence of related arguments rather than as separate topics. Ahmed et al. (2015) provide the basic mechanism: when exports contain more foreign value added, exchange-rate changes have a weaker effect on export volumes because the imported-input channel partly offsets the final-goods competitiveness channel. This is the foundation of the proposal.

de Soyres et al. (2021) and Adler et al. (2023) make the mechanism more complex. They show that GVCs do not only change the size of imported-input dependence. They also change where value is produced, where it is absorbed, and how trade flows adjust to exchange-rate movements. Their work is useful because it suggests that a single country-level or manufacturing-level elasticity may hide different responses across production structures.

The product-sophistication and ICT studies then move the discussion closer to the sector studied here. Chen et al. (2023) show that more sophisticated Chinese exports are less responsive to exchange-rate changes. Chen et al. (2026) find a similar pattern for ICT exports. These results do not directly prove that C26 has a weaker exchange-rate elasticity, but they support the idea that technologically complex and ICT-related exports may not respond to exchange rates in the same way as less complex goods.

The East Asian electronics literature helps connect this logic to production networks. Thorbecke (2018) shows the close relationship between electronics exports and electronics imports in ASEAN, while Thorbecke (2019) discusses exchange rates in East Asian value chains. Xing (2012) shows how China’s processing imports from East Asia are linked to finished exports to third markets. These studies make the imported-input channel more concrete for electronics-related production.

Putting these strands together gives the research logic of this proposal. Ahmed et al. (2015) explain why backward GVC participation should reduce exchange-rate elasticity. de Soyres et al. (2021) and Adler et al. (2023) explain why value-chain structure matters for adjustment. Chen et al. (2023, 2026) show that sophisticated and ICT-related exports can

be less responsive to exchange rates. Thorbecke (2018, 2019) and Xing (2012) show why electronics production in Asia is closely tied to imported inputs and regional supply chains. C26 sits at the intersection of these arguments.

The proposal therefore does not treat the literature review as background only. The reviewed studies lead directly to the empirical comparison. If backward GVC participation dampens exchange-rate elasticity in manufacturing overall, and if C26 is more strongly connected to imported components and fragmented electronics production, then it is reasonable to test whether the dampening effect is stronger in C26 than in broader manufacturing. That is the link between the literature and the model used in the methodology.

The same synthesis also helps define the limits of the proposal. Ahmed et al. (2015) justify using backward GVC participation as the main moderating variable. de Soyres et al. (2021) and Adler et al. (2023) justify paying attention to value-chain structure rather than treating exchange rates as a simple bilateral price variable. Chen et al. (2023, 2026) justify expecting weaker responsiveness in more sophisticated or ICT-related exports. Thorbecke (2018, 2019) and Xing (2012) justify the focus on East Asian electronics production and the interpretation of China and Malaysia. None of these studies alone gives the exact research question, but together they point toward it.

This is why the proposal avoids adding too many extra variables at the conceptual stage. The central relationship is already demanding: exchange rates, exports, backward GVC participation, and sectoral comparison. Adding more channels, such as tariffs, firm productivity, or invoicing currency, could make the proposal less focused and harder to complete. Those channels may matter, but they are better treated as part of the interpretation or limitations unless suitable data are available within the master’s thesis timeframe.

The literature therefore supports a focused empirical design. The study can remain narrow and still be meaningful because the narrowness comes from the research gap itself. It does not ask every possible question about exchange rates and GVCs. It asks whether one established mechanism becomes stronger in a sector where the production structure makes that outcome plausible.

## 6. Conceptual Framework and Hypotheses

The framework begins from the expected negative relationship between REER appreciation and exports. A higher REER means real appreciation, so exports should normally fall because foreign-market price competitiveness weakens.

Backward GVC participation changes this relationship. When a sector uses more imported inputs, exchange-rate movements affect both export prices and input costs. The expected export response should therefore be weaker when backward participation is higher. In the empirical model, this is captured through the interaction between the REER and lagged backward

GVC participation.

C26 is expected to show a stronger moderation effect than broader manufacturing. The reason is not simply that C26 is a “high-tech” sector. The more relevant point is that electronics-related production often depends on imported components, specialized inputs, and cross-border production stages. In that setting, exchange-rate movements are less likely to work only through the final-export price channel.

The conceptual framework can therefore be summarized in three linked relationships. First, REER appreciation is expected to reduce exports through weaker price competitiveness. Second, backward GVC participation is expected to soften that relationship because imported-input costs move with exchange rates. Third, the softening effect is expected to be stronger in C26 because the sector is more closely tied to imported components and fragmented electronics production than average manufacturing.

This framework is deliberately simple. It does not claim that backward GVC participation is the only reason export elasticities differ across sectors. Demand conditions, product quality, market power, invoicing currency, and trade policy may also matter. The purpose here is to isolate one channel that can be measured consistently with available data and that follows clearly from the literature. Keeping the framework focused also makes the empirical test easier to interpret.

The hypotheses are:

- **H1:** REER appreciation is associated with lower industry-level exports.
- **H2:** Higher backward GVC participation weakens the negative effect of REER appreciation on industry-level exports.
- **H3:** The moderating effect of backward GVC participation is stronger in C26 manufacturing than in broader manufacturing.
- **H4:** The interpretation of the moderating pattern differs between China and Malaysia
because their industrial structures and forms of imported-input dependence differ.

## 7. Methodology

### 7.1 Research Design

The empirical design uses annual country-industry panel data. The unit of analysis is the country-industry-year observation. The project is not set up as a simple China-Malaysia comparison. Instead, the wider panel is used to estimate the relationship between exchange rates, backward GVC participation, and exports. China and Malaysia are then discussed as focal cases when interpreting the results.

This design is useful because a two-country sample would be too narrow for the main estimation. A broader panel provides more variation across countries, industries, and years.

At the same time, the focal-case discussion keeps the research connected to actual production-network settings rather than leaving the results at a purely abstract level.

### 7.2 Data Sources

The main data sources are:

1. OECD Trade in Value Added (TiVA): used for country-industry exports and backward
GVC participation measures.

2. Bank for International Settlements (BIS) effective exchange-rate data: used for
REER measures.

3. Supplementary macroeconomic data, if needed: possible controls may include foreign
demand or country-level macroeconomic indicators, depending on coverage and consistency.

The sample period will depend on the overlap between TiVA and BIS REER coverage. C26 will be identified as computer, electronic, and optical products. The broader manufacturing benchmark will be built from available manufacturing industries, preferably excluding C26 when the data allow it.

### 7.3 Data Construction and Sample Design

The panel will be constructed by matching OECD TiVA country-industry-year observations with BIS REER data. The TiVA data provide the industry-level export values and the foreign value-added share embodied in exports. The BIS data provide country-year REER measures. Since the REER is measured at the country-year level, it will be matched to each industry observation within the same country and year.

The first step in the data construction will be to define the TiVA country-industry-year sample. For each country and year, manufacturing industries will be identified according to the TiVA industry classification. C26 will be coded as the core sector. The broader manufacturing comparison group will include other manufacturing industries, and the preferred definition will exclude C26 so that the comparison is not partly mechanical. If data limitations make this difficult in some parts of the sample, the definition including C26 can be kept as a robustness check rather than the preferred benchmark.

The second step will be to construct the main variables. Industry-level gross exports will be converted into logarithms. REER will also be converted into logarithms, with a higher value interpreted as appreciation. Backward GVC participation will be measured as the foreign value-added share embodied in exports. Because the model uses lagged backward participation, the first available year for each country-industry series may be lost in the regression sample.

The third step will be to check the overlap between TiVA and BIS coverage. Some countries may appear in one dataset but not the other, and some industry-year observations may

be missing. The final sample will therefore be an unbalanced panel if missing observations are not systematic enough to justify dropping entire countries or industries. An unbalanced panel is acceptable for this type of study, but the proposal will report the sample coverage clearly so that the reader can see which countries, industries, and years are included.

Missing values will not be filled mechanically. If export values, foreign value-added shares, or REER data are missing, those observations will be excluded from the relevant regression. This is safer than interpolating key variables, because interpolation could create artificial smoothness in variables that are central to the research question. If a large number of observations are lost for particular countries or years, this will be reported as part of the data limitation.

The sample design also needs to keep the role of China and Malaysia clear. They are not the only countries in the regression sample. They will be included in the broader panel where data are available, and then discussed more closely in the interpretation stage. This makes the empirical design stronger than a two-country regression while still allowing the thesis to focus on two economies that are highly relevant to Asian electronics production.

Before estimation, the dataset will also be checked for basic consistency. Export values should be positive before logarithms are taken. If zero or missing export values appear, those observations cannot be used in the log specification and will need to be excluded or discussed separately. Backward GVC participation should fall within a sensible range because it is measured as a share. Extreme or implausible values will be inspected rather than automatically removed, since they may reflect either real structural differences or data problems.

The construction of the broader manufacturing benchmark is especially important. If C26 is included in the benchmark, the comparison becomes less clean because the treatment sector is partly contained in the comparison group. For that reason, the preferred benchmark will exclude C26. However, the thesis can also report a version using all manufacturing as a robustness check, since some readers may want to know whether the result depends on how the benchmark is defined.

The panel structure also means that there may be repeated observations for the same country-industry pair across years. This is useful because it allows the fixed-effects model to compare changes within the same country-industry unit over time, rather than relying only on cross-sectional differences between countries or industries. The country-industry fixed effects are therefore important for reducing the influence of time-invariant structural differences, such as long-standing industrial specialization or geographic position.

The final data section of the thesis will report the sample period, number of countries, number of industries, number of country-industry-year observations, and the coverage of C26 compared with the broader manufacturing group. It will also report descriptive statistics for exports, REER, and backward GVC participation. These descriptive results will not answer the research question by themselves, but they will help show whether the empirical sample is suitable for the models.

### 7.7 Expected Signs and Interpretation Strategy

The expected sign of 𝛽1 is negative. Since a higher REER means appreciation, a negative coefficient would mean that appreciation is associated with lower exports. This would be consistent with the standard competitiveness channel. If 𝛽1 is statistically weak or has the opposite sign, the result would not automatically invalidate the project, but it would suggest that the baseline exchange-rate channel is weaker in the sample or that other factors dominate the average relationship.

```text
The expected sign of 𝛽3 is positive. In the moderation model, 𝛽3 shows whether backward GVC participation changes the effect of the REER on exports. If 𝛽1 is negative and 𝛽3 is
```

positive, then higher backward participation makes the negative effect of appreciation smaller in absolute value. This would match the imported-input cost channel: industries with more foreign value added respond less strongly to exchange-rate changes.

If 𝛽3 is close to zero, the interpretation would be different. It would mean that backward participation does not clearly moderate the REER-export relationship in the sample, even if the baseline exchange-rate effect exists. If 𝛽3 is negative, that would suggest that higher backward participation strengthens the negative effect of appreciation rather than weakening it. Such a result would need careful discussion, because it would not fit the main hypothesis. Possible explanations could include differences in pricing behavior, demand conditions, or the possibility that imported-input dependence is not the dominant channel in the sample.

The key coefficient in the C26 comparison is 𝛾6. The expected sign is positive if C26 has a stronger dampening effect than broader manufacturing. In practical terms, a positive 𝛾6 would mean that the interaction between REER and backward GVC participation is more offsetting in C26 than in the comparison group. That would support H3.

If 𝛾6 is not statistically significant, the study would still be informative. It would suggest that C26 does not differ clearly from broader manufacturing in the moderating role of backward participation, at least within the available data. If 𝛾6 is negative, the result would go against the expected sectoral argument and would require a more cautious interpretation. It may mean that C26’s export response is shaped by other channels not captured by the model, or that the broader manufacturing benchmark already contains sectors with similar imported-input dependence.

The interpretation will therefore not rely only on whether the signs match the hypotheses. It will also consider the size of the marginal effects, the difference between C26 and broader man-

ufacturing, and whether the results remain stable in robustness checks. For China and Malaysia, the interpretation will focus on whether the estimated pattern is consistent with their roles in electronics and semiconductor-related production networks.

The marginal effects will be important because interaction coefficients are not always easy to read directly. A positive interaction term does not mean exports rise with appreciation. It means the negative effect of appreciation becomes smaller as backward participation increases. For that reason, the thesis will explain the results in terms of the implied REER elasticity at different levels of backward GVC participation, rather than only reporting the coefficient table.

For the C26 comparison, the interpretation will focus on the difference between 𝛾3 and

```text
𝛾3 +𝛾6 . If 𝛾3 is positive but 𝛾6 is small, then backward participation may dampen exchange-rate
```

elasticity in manufacturing generally, but not especially in C26. If both 𝛾3 and 𝛾6 are positive, the evidence would be closer to the main argument. If 𝛾3 is weak but 𝛾6 is positive, the result would suggest that the dampening effect is mainly visible in C26 rather than across broader manufacturing.

The China and Malaysia discussion will be used carefully. It will not claim that the model estimates a separate causal effect for each country unless separate estimates are actually run. Instead, the focal-case discussion will relate the estimated sectoral pattern to known production structures. For example, if C26 shows weaker REER responsiveness at higher backward participation, the discussion can ask whether this is consistent with the role of imported components in China’s and Malaysia’s electronics-related exports.

### 7.8 Robustness Checks

Several robustness checks will be considered:

1. Estimating the moderation model separately for C26 and the broader manufacturing
benchmark.

2. Defining broader manufacturing both including and excluding C26, with the exclusion
version preferred where feasible.

3. Testing alternative lags for backward GVC participation if the sample size allows.
4. Adding macroeconomic controls such as foreign demand or GDP indicators if data coverage is sufficient.
5. Checking whether the results are sensitive to influential countries or unusual crisis years.
These checks are meant to test the stability of the pattern. They do not turn the design into a full causal identification strategy.

### 7.9 Research Boundaries and Feasibility

The analysis remains at the country-industry level because TiVA measures backward participation at that level. This is a boundary of the study, but it is also a practical strength. The

research question is about industry-level export exchange-rate elasticity under different degrees of backward GVC participation, so a country-industry-year panel is appropriate for the main purpose.

Firm-level data would allow a more detailed analysis of pricing, imported-input use, and firm heterogeneity. However, such data are difficult to obtain consistently across countries and are not necessary for the main comparison in this proposal. Product-level trade data could also provide more detail, especially for ICT goods or semiconductor-related products, but product-level exports do not align easily with TiVA-based backward GVC measures. Combining product-level trade data with industry-level value-added data would create measurement problems that may be too large for a master’s thesis.

For that reason, the study does not claim to explain individual firm behavior or product-level export responses. It focuses instead on structural patterns across countries, industries, and years. The results should be interpreted as evidence about sectoral differences in the moderating role of backward GVC participation, not as a complete causal account of every export decision.

China and Malaysia are focal cases rather than the whole sample because a two-country design would be too limited for the econometric part of the project. A broader panel provides more variation and makes the estimates more credible. At the same time, China and Malaysia are too important to leave as ordinary observations only. Their roles in Asian electronics production make them useful for interpreting what the sectoral results mean in practice.

C26 is also an aggregate category. Computer, electronic, optical, and semiconductor-related activities are not identical, and the category may include products with different levels of complexity and imported-input dependence. Still, C26 is a workable and meaningful category because it is available in the TiVA classification and captures a sector where imported inputs and cross-border production are likely to be more important than in average manufacturing.

The design is feasible for a master’s thesis because it uses accessible secondary data, a clear set of variables, and a limited number of empirical models. The models are extensions of standard fixed-effects panel regressions rather than a fully new econometric framework. The main challenge will be careful data construction and interpretation, not collecting new data from firms or conducting fieldwork. This makes the project manageable while still allowing it to make a focused contribution.

The feasibility also comes from the way the research question is bounded. The thesis does not need to estimate every possible channel through which exchange rates affect exports. It focuses on one measurable channel, backward GVC participation, and one sectoral comparison, C26 versus broader manufacturing. This gives the project enough depth without making the empirical design too large.

Another practical advantage is that the main data sources are public or institutionally accessible. OECD TiVA and BIS REER data are widely used in trade and macroeconomic research. The work required is mainly data cleaning, matching, variable construction, and panel

estimation. These tasks are demanding but realistic within a master’s thesis schedule.

The main risk is not data collection but interpretation. Because the design is not a natural experiment, the results should be discussed as conditional associations consistent with the proposed mechanism, not as definitive causal proof. Being clear about this boundary will make the thesis stronger. It allows the study to make a real contribution while remaining honest about what the data and model can support.

## 8. Expected Contribution and Significance

The contribution is mainly sectoral. Rather than repeating the general claim that GVC participation weakens exchange-rate elasticity, the project asks whether the effect is stronger in C26 manufacturing.

It also brings together several related discussions: GVC participation and exchange-rate elasticity, value-chain structure, and product sophistication or ICT exports. These topics often point in the same direction but are not always connected in one empirical design. C26 provides a way to connect them without making the argument too broad.

The China and Malaysia discussion adds practical interpretation to the panel results. Both countries are linked to Asian electronics production, but they do not occupy the same position in the region. This makes them useful cases for thinking through what the estimated coefficients may mean in actual production settings.

The policy relevance is also worth noting. If C26 exports are less responsive to exchange-rate movements because of backward GVC participation, then exchange-rate adjustment alone may have limited effects on export performance in electronics-related sectors. Supply-chain access, imported-input costs, industrial upgrading, and technology capability may be just as important for competitiveness.
